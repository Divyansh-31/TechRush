Piano Notes Overview

In classical and popular music, especially from the Western world, there are twelve different notes. Seven of these notes are called the natural notes and are represented by the white keys on the piano. The black keys represent the remaining five notes.

1. Natural Notes

The natural notes are named after the first seven letters of the alphabet: A, B, C, D, E, F, and G. The names of the notes are always capitalized.

The piano is a helpful aid because it provides a visual overview of the notes. The starting point is usually based on the note C.

The black keys are arranged in alternating groups of two and three. The note C is always on the white key to the left of the group of two black keys. Each note appears in several places on the piano: the further right, the higher the pitch; the further left, the lower the pitch.

Exercise: Try identifying “Naturals 1” and “Naturals 2” on a piano diagram.

2. Middle C

The middlemost C on the piano is called Middle C. On a standard 88-key piano, Middle C is the fourth C from the left. It serves as a key reference point to identify the correct version of a note.

3. Octaves

A standard piano has seven to eight versions of each note. The distance from any note to the nearest higher or lower version of the same note is called an octave.

Octaves are numbered from low to high, starting with C. The octave containing the lowest C is called octave 1 (C1), while the octave containing the highest C is octave 8 (C8). All notes from C up to the next C share the same number.
